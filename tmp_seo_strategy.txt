India-Wide SEO Strategy for FLOW by Crescora
Executive summary
FLOW should not try to win India by chasing only broad head terms like “chatbot platform” or “WhatsApp
API.” The higher-probability path is to build a state × workflow × industry search architecture: state hubs
for every Indian state and UT, workflow pages for the jobs buyers already search for , and industry pages for
the verticals most likely to adopt conversational automation first. That approach fits both the market and
the search landscape. India had 1,028.61 million internet subscribers at the end of December 2025,
including 1,007.35 million broadband subscribers; meanwhile, Meta says 91% of online adults in India
chat with a business weekly, and its new India rollout explicitly highlights appointments, payments, lead
capture, and human takeover inside WhatsApp. 
The commercial opportunity is not evenly distributed. Using the Government of India’s state/UT-wise
Udyam registration data as a proxy for business density, the top twelve states in this report account for
roughly 81% of registered MSME base; Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, West Bengal, Karnataka,
Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, Gujarat, Bihar , Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, and Odisha should therefore
receive the first wave of bespoke SEO investment. The same MSME report also shows that trading and
services together make up about 79% of registrations, which is a strong reason to prioritize workflow
pages for support, reminders, appointments, billing, payments, and document collection ahead of purely
technical AI content. 
Language matters, but not every language needs a full-site rollout on day one. Google’s multilingual
guidance supports language and region variants through hreflang, and India’s language internet usage
has long been shifting toward Indian languages rather than English-only consumption. At the same time,
Google Search Console and Google Trends are the right tools to validate whether commercial SaaS demand
appears in English, local script, or mixed transliteration. The practical rollout is: English everywhere, then
localized modules and priority pages in Hindi, Bengali, Tamil, Telugu, Marathi, Gujarati, Kannada,
Malayalam, Punjabi, Odia, and Assamese, beginning with the highest-value workflow pages and top-
priority states. 
Technically, FLOW should behave like a modern SaaS site built for both classical search and AI-assisted
search: crawlable HTML, disciplined canonicalization, multilingual structure only where it is maintainable,
structured data that helps Google understand software and page hierarchy, and state pages that are
genuinely distinct rather than mass-spun. Google’s current documentation is explicit that “AEO/GEO” is still
SEO, and that teams should focus on technical clarity plus unique, non-commodity content rather than
gimmicks like llms.txt or thin fan-out pages. 
Market context and assumptions
This strategy uses a primary-source-first approach: Google Search Central documentation, Google Search
Console guidance, Google Ads Keyword Planner documentation, Meta and WhatsApp official

Google Keyword Planner , Ahrefs, Semrush, and FLOW’s own Search Console exports were not available

The state-priority model in this report is based on four inputs: state/UT-wise Udyam registrations as a
proxy for business base, India’s very large broadband and mobile search addressable market, strong
messaging usage for business interaction, and the continued importance of Indian-language internet
demand. That is why the report favors long-tail commercial pages such as “whatsapp automation
maharashtra” and “clinic appointment booking software tamil nadu” over generic awareness-only pages. 
Two practical assumptions drive the language plan. First, only the user-specified languages are covered
here: Hindi, Bengali, Tamil, Telugu, Marathi, Gujarati, Kannada, Malayalam, Punjabi, Odia, and
Assamese. Languages outside that scope, such as Urdu, Konkani, Manipuri, Khasi, Mizo, and Nepali, are
noted when relevant but not expanded into full keyword sets here. Second, for B2B SaaS queries, many
Indian users still search in English or mixed transliteration; therefore, local-language seeds below should be
treated as launch hypotheses, then refined with Search Console query data and Google Trends geography
splits after pages go live. 
A tight rollout is therefore better than a maximal rollout. The recommendation is to launch:
a national India hub,
bespoke pages for the top twelve states first,
national workflow pages for every core workflow,
industry pages for hospitals, clinics, real estate, support teams, and service businesses,
then state-workflow and state-industry combinations only where there is enough unique proof, FAQ
content, partner context, or case study material to avoid thin duplication. Google’s own guidance
warns against scaled content patterns that exist mainly to manipulate ranking visibility. 
Keyword architecture across workflows and states
The keyword system should be built in layers, not as one flat list. Start with workflow pages that target
recurring buyer jobs. Then combine those with state modifiers for regional commercial demand. Finally,
add industry modifiers where the workflow is especially obvious to the buyer , such as hospital
appointments, clinic reminders, real estate lead qualification, and service-business support. Google says
topical organization in directories helps large sites, and internal links remain critical to discovery and
context.

They are intentionally conservative and should be replaced with exact exports from Google Keyword
Planner , Ahrefs, Semrush, and Search Console before quarterly planning.

Site architecture, landing pages, and templates
The architecture should be hub-and-spoke, with the India hub feeding state hubs, then workflow and
industry combinations. That gives Google a clean topical graph and gives buyers a predictable route from
broad problem to local relevance to category fit.
Google’s documentation supports clear topical organization, descriptive titles, useful snippets, strong
internal linking, and directories that group similar topics. It also notes that keywords in paths and TLD
choices are weak signals compared with content clarity and structure, so FLOW should choose a simple URL
system and keep it stable.

Open questions and limitations
The biggest limitation is keyword precision. This report does not have a live export from Google Keyword
Planner , Ahrefs, Semrush, or FLOW’s Search Console, so all volume and competition figures are modeled
ranges for prioritization rather than exact live metrics. Before implementation, export exact data for:
each Tier A state,
each core workflow,
each target language,
and the top competitor comparison terms. 
The second limitation is language form. Some commercial demand in India will appear in English, some in
local script, and some in transliterated English. The correct split can only be confirmed with live Search
Console and Google Trends data after initial launch. 
The final implementation rule is straightforward: do not publish thin state pages. If a page cannot carry
unique proof, workflow detail, localized FAQ, or local commercial context, hold it until it can. That is the
difference between a defensible India-wide SEO program and a large but fragile page farm.
